Heart attack (MI)

A heart attack happens when blood flow to the heart is substantially decreased or blocked. The term "myocardial infarction" also refers to a heart attack.

  • Chest pain that may feel like pressure, tightness, agony, squeezing, or hurting
  • Pain or discomfort spreads to the shoulder, arm, back, neck, jaw, teeth, or upper belly.
  • Cold sweat
  • breathing difficulty

Acute coronary syndrome

Acute coronary syndrome is a term that refers to a number of ailments connected to suddenly decreased heart blood flow.

  • Chest pain (angina), is often described as hurting, pressure, tightness, or burning
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • D, lightheadedness, or fainting
  • Feeling anxious or restless

Angina-Stable Angina

Stable angina (angina pectoris) is a type of chest discomfort that occurs when your heart muscle requires more oxygen than usual but is unable to obtain it due to cardiac disease.

  • Pain that spreads to your left shoulder or down your left arm
  • Breathing difficulty
  • Dizziness
  • Nausea
  • Exhaustion

Unstable angina

Unstable angina is a condition that occurs when your heart does not receive enough blood and oxygen. It could result in a heart attack.

  • Chest pain may radiate to other parts of your upper body and feel tight or crushing.
  • Even when not exerting yourself, you experience chest pain.
  • Shortness of breath
  • Chest pain that doesn’t stop after you take medicine.

NSTEMI

Non-ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction is a type of heart attack, mostly referred to as NSTEMI or a non-STEMI.

  • Tension or stiffness in your chest
  • Neck discomfort or annoyance
  • Nausea or a queasy feeling
  • Increased sweating (unexplained)

STEMI

Heart attacks that are classified as ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) are more severe and carry a higher risk of fatal consequences.

  • Respiration difficulty.
  • Fatigue.
  • Cold sweat
  • Vomiting and nauseous.

Heart failure

Heart failure, often referred to as congestive heart failure, happens when the heart muscle is unable to pump blood as smoothly as it should.

  • Legs, ankles, and feet swelling
  • Irregular or fast heartbeat
  • Reduced ability to exercise
  • Swelling of the belly area (abdomen)

Arrhythmia

An irregular heartbeat is known as arrhythmia. It indicates that your heart is not beating normally.

  • Palpitations (a feeling of skipped heartbeats, fluttering, or "flip-flops")
  • Pounding in your chest
  • Fainting or exhaustion (feeling very tired)
  • Anxiousness and blurry vision

Atrial fibrillation

A-fib, or atrial fibrillation, is irregular and frequently very rapid heart rhythm (arrhythmia) that can cause heart blood clots.

  • The sensation is like your heart beating rapidly, fluttering, or pounding.
  • Chest pain
  • Fatigue
  • Weakness

Cardiomyopathy

Heart muscle disorder known as cardiomyopathy makes it more difficult for the heart to pump blood throughout the body.

  • Breathing difficulty with activity or even at rest
  • Legs, ankles, and feet swelling
  • Stomach bloating is caused by a fluid buildup
  • Cough while lying down

Rheumatic heart disease

Heart valve damage brought on by rheumatic fever is known as rheumatic heart disease. Your body's inflammatory response to a bacterial infection is fever.

  • Chest pain
  • Heart fluttering
  • Breathing difficulty.
  • Stomach, hands, or feet swelling

Hypertension

In hypertension, which is a common condition, the blood's long-term strain against your artery walls is so great that it may eventually result in health issues including heart disease.

  • Sweating
  • Anxiety
  • Sleeping issues
  • Severe headaches

Iron anemia

Anemia, a disorder in which the blood does not contain sufficient healthy red blood cells, is frequently caused by iron deficiency. The body's tissues receive oxygen through red blood cells.

  • Extreme fatigue
  • Pale skin
  • Brittle nails
  • Unusual desires for things like ice, dirt, or starch that are not food.

Megaloblastic anemia

A type of macrocytic anemia called megaloblastic anemia develops when the bone marrow creates stem cells that result in excessively large red blood cells.

  • Muscle weakness
  • Skin that is paler than usual
  • Swollen tongue, or glossitis
  • Smooth or tender tongue

Thalassemia

A genetic blood disorder called thalassemia makes your body create less hemoglobin than is normal.

  • Pale or yellowish skin
  • Facial bone abnormalities
  • Abdominal swelling
  • Dark urine

Sickle cell anemia

One of the genetic diseases known as sickle cell disease is sickle cell anemia. Red blood cells, which deliver oxygen to every region of the body, are affected in terms of structure.

  • Excessive fatigue or irritability, from anemia
  • Fussiness in babies
  • Bedwetting brought on by related kidney issues
  • Yellowing of the eyes and skin is referred to as jaundice.

Platelet disorders

When your blood's platelet count is overly high, or incredibly low, or your platelets are not functioning properly, you can develop platelet disorders.

  • Frequent and difficult-to-stop nosebleeds
  • Petechiae are tiny red spots on the skin that are brought on by bleeding.
  • Gum bleeding
  • Heavy menstruation cycles for women that extend more than seven days

Leukemia-AML

Cancer of the blood and bone marrow is known as acute myeloid leukemia (AML). In adults, it is the most prevalent form of acute leukemia.

  • Bone pain
  • Fatigue and lethargy
  • Pale skin
  • Frequent infections

Leukemia-ALL

Acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL) is a form of blood and bone marrow cancer. Blood cells are created in the spongy tissue inside of bones.

  • Having gum bleeding
  • Frequent or severe nosebleeds
  • lumps in and around the neck, armpits, belly, or groin brought on by enlarged lymph nodes
  • Weakness, tiredness, or a general decrease in energy

Leukemia-CML

A rare form of bone marrow cancer, or chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML), the spongy tissue inside bones wherein blood cells are generated, can occur. The amount of white blood cells in the blood rises as a result of CML.

  • Feeling full after eating a small amount of food
  • Feeling run-down or exhausted
  • Loss of weight without effort
  • Excessive sweating during sleep (night sweats)

Leukemia-CLL

A kind of blood cancer called chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) affects the bone marrow, the soft tissue found inside bones where blood cells are created.

  • Painless, yet enlarged, lymph nodes
  • Upper left abdominal pain that an oversized spleen could bring on
  • Night sweats
  • Frequent infections

Myeloma

Cancer known as myeloma occurs in a type of white blood cell known as a plasma cell. Healthy plasma cells produce antibodies that bind to and destroy bacteria, assisting your body in fighting illnesses.

  • Bone pain, especially in your spine or chest
  • Constipation
  • Loss of appetite
  • Mental fogginess or confusion

Lymphoma

A cancer of the lymphatic system, which is a part of the body's defense against infection, is lymphoma.

  • Painless swelling of lymph glands in your neck, underarms, or groin
  • Persistent fatigue
  • Fever
  • sweats at night

Bleeding and Clotting Disorders

The normal way your blood clots is affected by a bleeding disorder. Blood turns from a liquid to a solid during the clotting process, commonly referred to as coagulation.

  • Swelling of the arm or leg
  • The touch of a leg or arm feels warm.
  • A lot of bruising
  • Excessive bleeding from tiny wounds or cuts
